# Video 5: Selling with Logic To Make Lots of Money
**Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iqAtBbGr3VY
**Speaker:** Alex Hormozi

## Core Concepts

1. **Logical Closing > Emotional Closing** — Emotional closes create buyer's remorse. Logical closes hold up when the excitement fades because the reasoning still stands two weeks later. Logic gives them something to anchor to when the feelings are gone.

2. **Sales = Helping People Make Decisions to Help Themselves** — Reframe selling from "getting someone to buy" to "helping someone decide." If they decide not to buy, that is also a win if it was an empowered, informed decision.

3. **Selling is a Transference of Belief Over a Bridge of Trust** — Both belief and trust are continuums, not binaries. The deeper your belief and the stronger the trust, the easier the transfer. If either is shallow, the sale collapses.

4. **The Three Sources of Powerlessness (Albert Ellis / CBT Framework)** — All objections trace back to people casting their power to one of three sources:
   - **Circumstances** — "I don't have the time / money / right situation"
   - **Other People** — "I need to talk to my spouse / partner / team"
   - **Self** — "I need to think about it" (avoidance out of fear of making a mistake)

5. **The Onion of Blame** — Objections are layered. Circumstances are the outermost layer, other people are the middle, self is the core. Expect to peel through 2-3 layers before reaching the real objection. Don't celebrate overcoming one layer — there are more underneath.

6. **Obstacles vs. Objections** — Obstacles happen BEFORE you present the offer (you're on offense, attacking limiting beliefs). Objections happen AFTER (they disagree with something about your offer). Handle as many obstacles as possible before presenting the offer so they don't blow up in the close.

7. **The 10-80-10 Rule** — 10% will never buy. 10% would buy from a scarecrow. 80% need help deciding. That 80% is what you train for.

8. **Closer or Further Framework** — The single most profitable decision framework: Don't ask "Will this get me what I want?" (requires too much predictive certainty). Ask "Will this get me closer or further from what I want?" This lowers the action threshold dramatically.

9. **Sales Ethics** — The difference between help and manipulation is intention. If the prospect knew everything you knew, they should want to buy. If they wouldn't, you're deceiving them, and that's why salespeople burn out.

## Key Techniques

### The Five Objection Categories & How to Overcome Each

#### 1. TIME — "Now's not a good time / I'm too busy"

**Macro (life is busy):**
- Trigger phrase in your head: "Busy is the best time"
- Logic: "You want to be [fit/successful] for the rest of your life, right? Do you think you'll ever NOT be busy? Then you should learn how to do this when you're busy. That's when you need the most support."
- The implied false statement: "I can only succeed when I'm not busy" — which means they'd quit every time life gets hard.

**Micro (don't have hours in the day):**
- Make yourself the relatable example: "I used to say the same thing to my wife. Then she grabbed my phone and showed me I spent 3.5 hours a day on social media."
- Social proof pressure: "Do you think there's anyone on Earth with less time than you who's been able to make this happen?"
- Reframe: "We're going to cut the 90% of what you're doing that isn't working. It's a net positive on time."

**When-Then Fallacy:**
- Name the fallacy directly: "That line of reasoning is called the when-then fallacy."
- Illustrate with absurdity: "It's like saying 'when I'm healed, I'll go to the hospital.' You go to the hospital to get healed. You go to the gym to get in shape. You don't come once you're already there."

#### 2. VALUE — "I can't afford it / That's a lot of money"

**Why "a lot of money" is GOOD for them:**
- "I'm actually more concerned when someone says it's NOT a lot of money — because they won't care enough. All my best testimonials come from people where this was a lot of money. If it's a lot of money for you, does that mean you're going to try really hard? Then you should be the last person worried about getting results."

**Why it's NOT actually a lot (value reframe):**
- "If you got [the result], would it be worth it? So the issue isn't whether it's expensive — it's whether you believe it can happen for you."
- Separate price from belief. Attack the belief separately.

**Don't let a bad decision burn you twice:**
- "You had something in the past that didn't work. The only thing worse than that bad experience is letting it burn you twice — once when it didn't work, and now a second time by preventing you from doing the right thing."
- Dating analogy: "Was your spouse the first person you ever dated? Imagine if you stopped dating after your first heartbreak."

**What's money good for anyway:**
- "You're going to spend this money in the next 12 months no matter what. The question is whether it'll be on something that pays dividends or more stuff you don't need."
- "You're going to buy these lessons either way — the question is whether you want to spend 15 years figuring it out or spend the money now."
- "We always spend the thing we value least. If you value time over money, you spend money to save time. Do I need to tell you which one rich people do?"

**Best Case / Worst Case Close:**
- "Both options are risk-free. You can walk out, or you can sign up with our guarantee. But only one of them gets you closer to your goal. No risk with no upside, or no risk with potential upside — which one do you want?"

**Resourcefulness, not resources:**
- "Self-made millionaires and you have something in common: both started at zero."
- Phil Knight / Nike story: He went to his vendors and asked them to pay his payroll. That's resourcefulness.
- "When you had an unexpected tax bill, you found the money. You flex that muscle for the IRS but not for yourself?"

#### 3. FIT — "Not sure it's for me"

**New Identity, New Priorities:**
- Sephora story: Girls become women, new identity creates new priorities. "We vote with our dollars for the things we care about. You say you care about this — but your bank account shows what you actually vote for."

**Pain of Change vs. Pain of Staying the Same:**
- Tony Robbins principle: When pain of staying the same exceeds pain of change, people change.
- Be direct: "Maybe you need to gain 50 more pounds and come back. You're not in enough pain yet." This provokes a reaction — "Well I don't want that" — "Then you're acting like you do."

**Hypothetical Isolate:**
- "If this program had everything you needed, would you do it?"
- "If it didn't have [the specific objection], would you do it?"
- Get agreement, then: "Great, we don't have to worry about that one thing."
- Alternative: "What is it missing?" — They usually can't answer, revealing the real issue is fear, not fit.

#### 4. AUTHORITY — "I have to talk to my spouse / partner"

Three-step process:

**Step 1 — Isolate and Collapse the Foil:**
- "What part do you think they wouldn't approve of?" The spouse isn't there, so the prospect answers with THEIR OWN real objection. Now you can address the actual issue, not the proxy.

**Step 2 — They already gave you permission:**
- "Do they approve of your current struggle?" (No.) "Then why would they be against something to fix what they already don't approve of?"
- Role reversal: "If the roles were reversed and THEY found something to help them, would you be against it?" (No.) "Why would it be different for them?"

**Step 3 — Permission vs. Support:**
- "Three years from now, still in the same position, and you blamed them for never letting you do it — is that fair to them? Is that fair to you?"
- "You've been looking for permission when what you really need is support. Do you think they'll support you solving this problem?"
- If still resistant: "We have a 3-day guarantee. If they really object, I'll tear it up. But of course they won't object — they want you to succeed."

#### 5. AVOIDANCE — "I need to think about it"

Translate internally: "I need to avoid this decision for fear of making a mistake."

**Past — Stack the evidence:**
- List everything they did to get here: "You saw the ad, you clicked it, you booked the call, you showed up. This isn't a fast decision — you've been deciding this for years. Today we're just taking the first step."
- "Do you think the reason you're in this position is because you haven't been able to pull the trigger? How much has NOT deciding cost you over the last five years?"
- "Do you want another year of almost?"

**Present — The Rocking Chair Close:**
- "You're not going to drive home, sit in a rocking chair, and thoughtfully evaluate this. You're going to check your phone, see 6 texts, get sucked into life, and five days from now put on something that doesn't fit and think 'I should have done that thing.'"
- "You don't need time to make a decision. You need information. I'm your source of information. What are the main things you're thinking through?"

**Decision Framework (walk them through it):**
- "Do you believe the product could work? Do you trust us? Do you think it'll work for you? Do you have access to the money? Great — that's the decision. Let's do it."
- People rarely make big decisions. You have to teach them HOW to decide.

**The Informed Decision Close (with guarantee):**
- "Can we agree you need to make an informed decision? How can you make an informed decision without actually doing it? Let's get you started — if you hate it in 30 days, I'll give your money back. At least you'll have made an informed decision instead of one based on what you imagine it might be."

**Future — Future Casting:**
- Negative: "Five years from now, doing what you're doing — are things better or worse?" Make them feel the weight of the trajectory.
- Positive: Both options are risk-free, only one gets you closer.
- "You're not going to struggle forever, right? If you're going to do this eventually, why not start now? Would you rather have the result for 3 years or 30?"

**Root of "Decide":**
- Latin "decidere" means "to kill off." "Which future are we killing off today — the one where things stay the same, or the one with everything you said you wanted?"

### Bonus Technique: The Mirror Close

Whatever reason the prospect gives you for NOT doing it, repeat it back as the reason they SHOULD do it:
- "I don't have the time" — "That's the exact reason you need this — so I can show you how to make the time."
- "I don't have the money" — "That's the exact reason you need this — because you need to get it."
- "My spouse doesn't support me" — "That's the perfect reason to do this."

Works because the thing they present with is the thing they're most afraid of — and it's usually the thing they most need to solve.

## Psychological Principles

1. **Loss Aversion via Future Casting** — People feel losses more intensely than gains. Project their current trajectory 5 years into the future to make the cost of inaction visceral. "You gained 50 lbs in 5 years. Tack that on. How does 300 feel?"

2. **Commitment & Consistency** — Stack all the micro-commitments they already made (clicked the ad, booked the call, showed up) to show that buying is the next consistent step, not a new decision.

3. **The Action Threshold** — Every technique is designed to LOWER the action threshold rather than increase pressure. Make the step feel smaller: "Will this get you closer or further?" requires less certainty than "Will this solve everything?"

4. **Cognitive Reframing (CBT)** — Directly name logical fallacies (when-then fallacy, letting a bad decision burn you twice). Naming the distortion gives people a handle to release the belief.

5. **Identity-Based Persuasion** — New identity creates new priorities. "You say you want to be [this person]. People who are [this person] invest in [this thing]. You vote with your dollars."

6. **Self-Attribution** — Make THEM feel like they made the decision. Don't fight or argue. Stay curious. "The goal of closing is to get someone to decide — not to buy." If they feel ownership of the decision, they follow through.

7. **Collapsing the Proxy** — When someone hides behind a third party (spouse, partner), ask what specifically the third party would object to. The prospect answers with their own objection, collapsing the proxy and putting them back in the driver's seat.

8. **Thermostat Resetting** — Everyone has an internal set point for what they'll tolerate (weight, income, fitness). You don't need more effort — you just need to reset the thermostat. Communicating that it's "made up in their head" creates a breakthrough moment.

9. **Reciprocal Empathy via Role Reversal** — "If the roles were reversed and your spouse found something to help them, would you be against it?" Forces empathy that dissolves the authority objection.

10. **Informed Decision Principle** — "You can't make an informed decision about something you've never done. The only way to truly know is to try it." Especially powerful with money-back guarantees.

## Application to AI Sales Agent

### Mapping Hormozi's Framework to the Forge Scanner Chat Agent

**1. Belief Transfer = Scan Results as Proof**
The AI agent doesn't need personal belief — it has DATA. The scan results, screenshots, annotations, and competitor comparisons ARE the proof. The agent should present findings as "it would be more unreasonable for you NOT to believe this" — pointing to the evidence on screen rather than making empty claims.

**2. The 5 Objection Categories Map Directly to Chat Responses:**

| Objection | AI Agent Response Strategy |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Time** — "I'm too busy to do a strategy call" | "That's exactly why this matters — you're too busy to also be losing leads from a broken funnel. A 30-minute call now saves you months of bleeding revenue. The busier you are, the more every lead counts." |
| **Value** — "I can't afford marketing right now" | "The scan found [X] issues costing you leads every day. Right now it costs you $[estimate] per year NOT fixing this. That's the real expense. The strategy call is free — we're just showing you where the money is leaking." |
| **Fit** — "I don't think this is for my type of business" | "Your scan shows the same patterns we fix for [similar businesses]. [Show data card with comparable results.] If the program addressed [specific concern], would you be open to exploring it? The call is specifically to see if it fits YOUR situation." |
| **Authority** — "I need to talk to my partner first" | "Totally get that. What part do you think they'd have questions about? ... Actually, that's exactly what the strategy call covers. Would it help to have them on the call too? That way you both get the full picture instead of playing telephone." |
| **Avoidance** — "Let me think about it" | "Makes sense. You don't need time though — you need information. What are the main things you're weighing? ... Your scan shows [X competitors] are already doing this. Every week without action is another week they're capturing leads that could be yours." |

**3. The Closer-or-Further Framework as Default Close:**
The AI agent should consistently use this low-pressure frame: "You don't have to commit to anything. The strategy call is just to see if we can get you closer to [their stated goal]. If we can't, you'll know in 30 minutes. If we can, you'll have a clear plan."

**4. Future Casting with Their Own Data:**
The scan gives the agent real numbers to project forward. "Your site loads in 6.2 seconds. Industry data shows you're losing ~40% of mobile visitors. Over the next 12 months at your current traffic, that's roughly [X] lost leads. That's the cost of not fixing this."

**5. The Mirror Close in Chat:**
When a prospect says "I don't have the budget for marketing," the agent should respond: "That's exactly why we should talk — because you can't afford to keep losing the leads you're already paying for. The scan shows [specific leak]. Fixing that costs less than the leads you're losing."

**6. Don't Let a Bad Decision Burn You Twice:**
When a prospect mentions past bad experiences with agencies: "I get it — you tried something before and it didn't work. The only thing worse than that is letting it prevent you from finding the right partner. That bad experience already cost you once — don't let it cost you the solution too. That's exactly why we lead with data, not promises. You can see the issues yourself in the scan."

**7. Curiosity Over Combat:**
The AI agent should NEVER argue or get defensive. Always respond to pushback with curiosity: "That's interesting — tell me more about that" or "Huh, what makes you say that?" This keeps the conversation open and peels back layers.

**8. Pain of Inaction > Pain of Action:**
The agent has a unique advantage: it can show the prospect's problems visually (annotated screenshots). Use the data to make inaction feel more painful than action: "Here's what your customers see when they land on your site. Every day this stays like this, you're making a first impression that pushes people away."

**9. The Informed Decision Close:**
"The strategy call is free and there's zero obligation. But it's the only way to make an informed decision about whether Forge can help you. Right now you're guessing — 30 minutes and you'll know for sure."

**10. Stack Their Micro-Commitments:**
"You ran the scan, you reviewed the results, you're here in the chat asking questions — clearly this matters to you. The strategy call is just the next step in a decision you've already been making."

**11. Resourcefulness Framing for Price Objections:**
Never accept "can't afford it" at face value. The scan proves there's money being left on the table. Reframe: "The question isn't whether you can afford to fix this — it's whether you can afford not to. Your scan shows you're paying for traffic that your site isn't converting."

**12. Personality & Tone:**
- Confident but not pushy — the data does the heavy lifting
- Curious and consultative — ask questions before prescribing
- Direct when needed — "Maybe you need to lose a few more leads before it hurts enough" (used sparingly, with earned trust)
- Always prioritize the prospect over the sale — if the data doesn't support a fit, say so honestly
- Use "closer or further" as the default decision frame
- Name patterns: "That's what I call the when-then trap" — gives the prospect language to release their own objection
