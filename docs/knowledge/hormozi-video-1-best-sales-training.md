## Core Concepts

### 1. Power = The Ability to Direct or Influence People and Events
- Power is neutral — it amplifies who you are. The goal of sales is to give the prospect power over their own decisions.
- Omnipotence is the ideal: zero distance between thought and reality. Every step toward that is more power. Help prospects close that gap.

### 2. Selling with Logic, Not Emotion
- Emotional buyers get excited, buy, then cancel days later. Logical buyers stick.
- "If you have a rational foundation for a decision, when the emotions fade, the logic will continue."
- Use logic to create emotion — walk someone through a framework that makes them feel powerful, not pressured.

### 3. Selling vs. Closing
- **Selling** happens the entire time, from first contact onward.
- **Closing** happens only after you ask for the sale. Everything after solicitation is closing.
- Red zone offense analogy: top 5 NFL red zone offenses made the playoffs 90% of the time. Closing ability compensates for deficiencies everywhere else.

### 4. The 10/80/10 Rule
- 10% of prospects will never buy (accept it).
- 10% will always buy (just don't screw it up).
- **The 80% in the middle is what you train for.** One extra close per month pays for everything.

### 5. Obstacles vs. Objections
- **Obstacle:** blocks progress BEFORE you ask for the sale. Easy to confront and destroy early.
- **Objection:** disagreement AFTER you ask for the sale. Harder to handle — this is closing territory.
- Handle obstacles early so you never face them as objections.

### 6. The Three Cognitive Distortions (from Dr. Albert Ellis / CBT)
All objections stem from three irrational beliefs:
1. **Circumstances:** "I must get what I want when I want it. If I don't, I can't stand it."
2. **Others:** "Other people must treat me fairly. If they don't, they deserve punishment."
3. **Self:** "I must do well, or else I am no good."

These manifest as five scapegoats prospects use to avoid deciding:
- **Time** (circumstances) — "Not a good time, I'm busy"
- **Value/Price/Money** (circumstances) — "Can't afford it, too expensive"
- **Fit** (circumstances/self) — "Not sure it's for me"
- **Authority** (others) — "Have to talk to my spouse/partner"
- **Avoidance** (self) — "Need to think about it"

### 7. The Onion of Blame
Prospects start by blaming circumstances (outermost layer), then blame other people, then finally confront themselves (core). You peel layers inward. If someone says "I need to think about it," you are already talking to the decision-maker — you just need them to decide.

### 8. Three Prerequisites for a Sale
The prospect must believe:
1. The product will get them to the goal **the way they want to get there**
2. You and others will support them (trust)
3. It will work for **them specifically**, not just everyone else

## Key Techniques

### Time Objection — Three Angles

**Macro (seasonal busy):**
> "You're going to be busy again in the future, right? So if you learn to do this while you're busy, you'll be able to do it forever. If you can only do it in perfect circumstances, you'll fall off when it gets busy again. Isn't that when you'd want the most support?"

**Micro (no time in the day):**
> Tell a relatable story — "My wife pulled out my phone and showed me 22 hours of social media. She said 'I guess I found your time.'" Then pivot: "The first thing a good program does is cut the 90% of stuff that isn't working. Jeff Bezos has the same 24 hours. It's not about adding to your plate — it's about removing what isn't working."

**When/Then Fallacy:**
> "This is called the When/Then Fallacy — 'When I have X, then I'll do Y.' That's like saying 'When I'm healthy, I'll go to the hospital.' Or 'I'll start saving money once I'm rich.' The program that makes you money is the thing you're waiting to have money for. It flips the sequence. It's a distortion — it's not real."

### Value/Price/Money Objection — Four Angles

**1. Why "a lot" is good:**
> "The fact that this is a lot of money to you is the exact reason you're going to be successful. You'll go all in. The best transformation stories always come from people in your exact situation."

**2. Relative value (sidestep to belief):**
> "If all this does is add $10K/month to your income, is it worth it? [Yes.] Then it's not about the price — it's about whether you believe me. So let's talk about that."

**3. Price anchoring with comparison:**
> "A four-year degree costs $50-200K and takes four years. This can help you make that amount in half the time for a tenth of the price."

**4. What's money good for anyway:**
> "You're going to spend this money either way over the next 12 months. And you're going to buy the program regardless — the question is whether you pay with money or pay with time. You've been paying with time for the last six years. How's that working?"

**5. Resourceful, not resources:**
> "Every self-made billionaire started with nothing — same seat you're in. The difference isn't resources, it's resourcefulness. You've had unexpected bills before — taxes, payroll, emergencies — and you figured it out. You're resourceful when it's for someone else. Why not for yourself?"

### Fit Objection — Three Angles

**1. New identity, new priorities:**
> Tell the Sephora story — little girls stepping into womanhood, budgeting for makeup because "this is what women do." Then: "You've had priorities aligned with your old identity. The you that goes out, drinks, spends on stuff that doesn't serve you. People who want to be rich spend money on education. Step into the new identity."

**2. Change the change:**
> "What you've been doing has gotten you what you've been getting. That breakfast that you insist on keeping? It's the one making you look like this. The question is: is the pain of staying the same greater than the pain of change?"

**3. Hypothetical close:**
> "If this were perfect, would you do it?" If they say yes: "What's different between perfect and what we've got?" They usually can't articulate it — proving it's not about the program, it's about them. If they name something specific and you can fix it, fix it and close: "Green beans instead of broccoli? Done. Sign here."

### Authority Objection — One Framework (Process)

**Sidestep the absent partner:**
> "What do you think they wouldn't like about it?" (They'll tell you — then overcome THAT objection, which has nothing to do with the spouse.)

**The full process:**
1. "Do they approve of your current struggle?" (No.)
2. "Then why would they oppose something that fixes what they already don't approve of?"
3. "If the roles were reversed and they needed this, would you support them? Then why wouldn't they support you?"
4. "The real problem is you're asking for **permission** instead of **support**. It's your life. If you give them that power and two years later nothing's changed, you'll resent them."
5. Frame the conversation they should have with their partner: "Instead of bitching about what's not working, I'm joining people who will help me fix it. I'm not asking permission. I'm asking for your support."

### Avoidance / "Need to Think About It" — Past, Present, Future

**Past-focused:**
> "This isn't a fast decision. You've been making this decision for six years. You've been struggling with this for how long? All we're deciding is whether you're actually going to do something about it."

**Don't let it burn you twice:**
> "You let a bad decision burn you twice — once when you made the bad investment, and a second time when you let it stop you from making a good one. That's like having a bad eighth-grade boyfriend and deciding men aren't for you."

**Pattern recognition:**
> "Do you think you're in this position because you've struggled to make decisions in the past? Have you been in this exact conversation and said no before? Do you think that's why you're still here? Then maybe this is the decision that changes that pattern."

**Present-focused — The Rocking Chair:**
> "You're not going to go home and sit in a rocking chair deliberating. You're going to pick up the kids, cook dinner, do laundry, and five days from now you'll put on old jeans that don't fit and nothing will have changed. Decisions don't take time — they take information. I'm the only source of information. I'm here now. Let's talk."

**The Three Decision Questions:**
1. "Do you believe this product will help you achieve what you want?"
2. "Do you trust me to fulfill what I said I'd do?"
3. "Do you think it will work for you?"
If yes to all three: "Do you have or have access to the money to get started?"

**Informed decisions require experience:**
> "How can you make an informed decision if you haven't even tried it? I'm not asking you to decide to buy right now — I'm asking you to make an informed decision, which you can only do from the inside. 30 days, if it's not what I said, money back."

**Etymology of "decide":**
> "Decide comes from Latin 'de-cadere' — to cut off, to kill. Which future are we killing today? The one where you achieve your dreams, or the one where you keep doing what you've been doing? Indecision IS a decision."

**Future-focused — Magnify the pain:**
> "It's been five years. How's another five sound? Are you tired of another year of almost? Almost hitting your goals, almost retiring your spouse, almost affording what you want?"

**Consider the options (with guarantee):**
> "Option 1: You do it, you get the result — awesome. Option 2: You don't do it, guaranteed no result. Option 3: You do it, it doesn't work — but we have a guarantee, so you're covered. Only one option has a true guarantee of failure: walking out the door."

**Urgency nudge:**
> "You're not going to struggle forever. You're going to fix this eventually. If you're going to do it anyway, you might as well start now so you enjoy the results sooner. Would you prefer making more money faster or slower?"

**Directional, not perfect:**
> "Do you think this will get you closer to your goal than what you're currently doing? We don't need to be snipers — we need to be directionally right. No one program saved my life. But the decision to invest in education changed it forever."

### The Universal Flip (Bonus Close)
> "The reason you're telling yourself NOT to do this is the exact reason you SHOULD. Can't afford it? That's the reason — how long do you want 'I can't afford it' on your list of problems? Don't have time? That's the reason — you need this MORE because you're drowning. Dependent on your spouse's approval? That's the reason — you need to own your decisions."

## Objection Handling Scripts

### Quick-Reference Decision Tree

| They say | Category | Core move |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| "Not a good time" | Time (macro) | "You'll always be busy. Best to learn while busy so it sticks." |
| "I don't have time in my day" | Time (micro) | "The program removes what isn't working. That IS the time." |
| "When I have X, then I'll Y" | Time (when/then) | "That's a logical fallacy. The program IS the thing that gives you X." |
| "Can't afford it" / "Too expensive" | Money | "If it does what we say, it's worth it — so it's not price, it's belief." |
| "I've been burned before" | Money/trust | "Don't let a bad decision burn you twice by blocking a good one." |
| "Not sure it's for me" | Fit | "If this were perfect, would you do it? [Yes.] Then what's missing?" |
| "I don't want to do X specific thing" | Fit | "If we removed that, would you do it? [Yes.] Done." |
| "Have to talk to my spouse" | Authority | "What would they not like?" Then overcome THAT. "It's support, not permission." |
| "Need to think about it" | Avoidance | "Decisions need information, not time. I'm the information. Let's talk now." |
| "I'm not sure" (vague) | Avoidance | Three questions: believe in product? Trust me? Think it works for you? |
| "What if it doesn't work?" | Avoidance/fear | "Three options. Only walking away guarantees failure." |

### Two Fastest Questions to Cut Through Anything
1. **"What are you most afraid of having happen if you buy?"** — Forces them to name the specific fear, which is almost always irrational once spoken aloud.
2. **"What's the worst thing you envision?"** — Then make it absurd: "I take your card, max it out, go to Vegas, put it all on black, flee to Monaco where they can't extradite me." They laugh, realize their fear is way less extreme, and then name the real concern.

## Psychological Principles

### 1. Belief Transfer Over a Bridge of Trust
Sales = transferring your belief to the prospect across a bridge of trust. Two requirements: (a) you must genuinely believe, and (b) they must trust you. Hot streaks end not from forgotten skills but from lost conviction. Fill your own cup first.

### 2. Conviction Is Contagious
"Anyone ever been around somebody who believes something so deeply — even if you think it's crazy — that you start questioning what YOU believe? That's what real conviction does." Deep belief in your product is more powerful than any technique.

### 3. Humans Detect Intention (Survival Mechanism)
"Humans are exceptionally good at sniffing out intention. It's a survival mechanism." If you only care about closing, they'll feel it. If you genuinely care about them, they'll feel that too.

### 4. Childlike Curiosity, Not Argumentation
"Seek to understand, not to argue." When someone objects: "Huh, that's interesting — tell me more about that." Maintain genuine curiosity. Fighters train to control breathing through exposure; salespeople train to stay calm through repetition.

### 5. The Prospect Is the Hero, Not You
"The more you can vanish in the sale and magnify them, the better." They don't care about you. Make it entirely about their life, their goals, their pain, their transformation.

### 6. Closing Is Seduction, Not Combat
"Closing is a dance, not a fight. It's seduction, not force." Sell from your back foot: "I'm good. I want to help you help you. If that means not doing this, I'm with you."

### 7. The Person Who Cares Most Wins
"The person who cares the most about the prospect wins the sale — that includes the prospect themselves." If you care more about their wellbeing than they do, you will close.

### 8. Sunk Cost as Momentum (Not Manipulation)
"You bought tickets, flew here, blocked the time, sat through this whole thing — you're six inches from gold. This isn't a fast decision at all." Use their existing investment as evidence of their desire, not as guilt.

### 9. Redefining the Metric
"You can win every sale because you change the metric you're measuring yourself by." Success = "Did I help them make a decision?" not "Did they buy?" This removes pressure and paradoxically increases close rates.

### 10. The Cost of Inaction
"The ignorance of not knowing how to create a million dollars a year is costing you a million dollars a year." Reframe doing nothing as the most expensive option. "The number one tax is the time tax of ignorance."

## Application to AI Sales Agent

The AI Sales Agent in the Forge scanner chat should embody these principles. Here is a mapping of each concept to the agent's behavior:

### Persona and Tone
- **Genuine care over closing pressure.** The agent should make the prospect feel like it cares more about their business succeeding than about booking a call. Mirror Hormozi's "I'm good either way — I want to help you help you."
- **Childlike curiosity.** When the prospect resists or deflects, respond with curiosity, not pushback: "That's interesting — what makes you say that?" or "Help me understand what you mean by that."
- **Conviction without aggression.** The agent has just analyzed their actual website/funnel. It has real data. It should speak with deep certainty about the findings — not hedging, not apologizing. "Your funnel has three critical gaps. Here's what they're costing you."
- **Sell from the back foot.** Never sound desperate. "I'm not here to convince you of anything. I just want to make sure you see what we found."

### Using the Scan Data as Proof
- Hormozi says: "Provide proof so that it'd be more unreasonable NOT to believe than to believe." The scan results ARE the proof. The agent should reference specific screenshots, specific annotation findings, specific scores. "Your homepage has no clear CTA above the fold. Your Google Business Profile has 12 reviews while your top competitor has 247. Your page loads in 6.2 seconds."
- This replaces "here's a thousand testimonials" with "here's YOUR data." Even more powerful because it's personalized.

### Handling "I Need to Think About It"
- **Rocking chair reframe:** "Totally understand. But here's the thing — these issues on your site are costing you leads right now. Every day you wait is another day of lost traffic converting at your current rate instead of what it could be."
- **Information, not time:** "What specific questions do you have? I've got your full analysis right here. A strategy call would walk you through exactly how to fix the three biggest issues — no commitment, just clarity."
- **Cost of inaction:** "Based on your traffic, the gaps we found are likely costing you [X] leads per month. That's [X] potential customers who visited and left."

### Handling "Can't Afford It" / Price Concerns
- **The strategy call is free.** Reframe: "The call itself costs nothing. Adrian just walks you through the findings and what fixing them would look like. You'd leave with a roadmap regardless."
- **When/Then Fallacy:** "A lot of business owners say they'll invest in their funnel once revenue picks up. But the funnel is what drives revenue. It's like saying you'll go to the hospital once you're healthy."
- **Relative value:** "If fixing your funnel brought in even 5 extra customers a month at your average deal size, what would that be worth over a year?"

### Handling "Not Sure It's for Me"
- **Hypothetical close:** "If you could wave a magic wand and your funnel was converting at 2x what it does today — would that matter to your business?"
- **Change the change:** "What you've been doing has gotten you the results you currently have. If you want different results, something has to change. The question is what."
- **Data-driven fit:** Reference their specific industry, their specific competitors, their specific gaps. Make it impossible to say "but my business is different" when you've literally scanned THEIR business.

### Handling "I Need to Talk to My Partner"
- **Sidestep and isolate:** "Totally — what do you think they'd want to know about it? I can make sure we cover all of that on the call so you're both informed."
- **Support not permission:** "The strategy call gives you a concrete plan you can bring back to them. Way easier to have that conversation with data than with a vague 'someone wants to talk to us about our website.'"

### The Universal Flip (Agent's Secret Weapon)
When a prospect names any reason not to book:
- "The fact that you're too busy to think about your funnel is exactly why it's broken — nobody's been watching it."
- "The fact that revenue is tight is exactly why you can't afford to keep leaking leads."
- "The fact that you've been burned by agencies before means you know what bad looks like — so you'll appreciate what good looks like even more."

### Timing and Rhythm
- The agent should not dump all this on someone at once. Hormozi peels layers like an onion. The agent should:
  1. **Lead with data** (the scan findings) — this is the "selling" phase.
  2. **Let the prospect react** — listen for which layer they're on.
  3. **Address one objection at a time** — peel, don't sledgehammer.
  4. **Always return to the CTA:** "Want me to set up a time for Adrian to walk you through this?"
